ICRA 2024 Tutorial on Ergodic Control

Ergodic control can be applied to diverse robots (flying, rolling or manipulating). It also targets a wide range applications in which some forms of exploration is required. In contrast to the stochastic sampling of points in an area to explore, ergodic control takes into account that there is a cost in moving from one sample to the next.

Ergodic control consists of moving within a spatial distribution by spending time in each part of the distribution in proportion to its density. The resulting controller generates natural exploration behaviors, which can be exploited for active sensing, localization, surveillance, insertion tasks, etc.

A conventional tracking problem in robotics is characterized by a target to reach, requiring a controller to be computed to reach this target. In ergodic control, instead of providing a single target point, a probability distribution is given to the robot, which must cover the distribution in an efficient way (namely, ``tracking a distribution'' instead of ``tracking a point'').

Ergodic control can be exploited in a wide range of problems requiring the automatic exploration of regions of interest. This is for example helpful when the available sensing information is not accurate enough to fulfill the task with a standard controller, but where this information can still guide the robot towards promising areas. In a collaborative task, it can be used when the operator's input is not accurate enough to fully reproduce the task, which then requires the robot to explore around the requested input (e.g., a point of interest selected by the operator). For picking and insertion problems, ergodic control can be applied to move around the picking/insertion point, thereby facilitating the prehension/insertion. It can also be employed for active sensing and localization (either detected autonomously, or with help by the operator). Here, the robot can plan movements based on the current information density, and can recompute the commands when new measurements are available (i.e., updating the spatial distribution used as target).

Several problem formulations and methods have been proposed for ergodic control, which will be presented and discussed during the tutorial. The problem has originally been formulated with a Spectral Multiscale Coverage (SMC) objective. Other ergodic control formulation has been proposed, formulated as a Heat Equation Driven Area Coverage (HEDAC) problem or with Kullback-Leibler divergence measures.
